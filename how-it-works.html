How This Works

The method. How you actually work with Claude day to day, how we run the weekly hour together, and the handful of tricks that make the difference between using an AI and pairing with one.

The Core Idea

AI writes SQL now. It builds dashboards. It cleans data. That part of analyst work is commodity.

What it can't do is know when a number is wrong. When a cash flow figure doesn't match what you'd expect for the quarter. When a customer cohort is missing because someone forgot to include it in the pull.

You already have that instinct. Years of treasury work gave it to you.

This course teaches two things. First, how to turn a business problem into a data question the AI can actually work on. Second, how to command the AI, read what it gives you, push back when it's wrong, and present the answer to people who don't care about the SQL.

Twelve weeks. You ship a portfolio piece every week. You do reps.

Set Up Your Claude Project

Twelve weeks, twelve Projects. One Claude Project per week so the context stays clean and the AI gets smarter about your work as the course moves.

Create the project

On claude.ai, click Projects in the sidebar, then Create Project. Name it something like Week 04 – SQL Basics. Keep the naming consistent across all twelve so you can find them fast.

Write the project instructions

This is the single highest-leverage thing you'll do. Put something like this in the instructions box:

I'm Stephen, working through Week [N] of a data analytics course. My background is treasury and accounting, so use treasury and finance examples when you explain things. I want to understand what every line of code does, don't just write it for me. When I ask a question, explain the thinking first and then give the code. When I get something wrong, tell me what I'm missing. Don't flatter me.

Update it every week. After Week 4 you can add "I know SQL basics, don't re-explain SELECT." After Week 7 you can add "I know Tableau, now I'm learning Looker Studio." The Project gets smarter as you do.

Upload your files

Drop every CSV, every dataset description, every course packet into the Project. Claude sees all of it in every conversation inside that Project. You don't have to re-upload for each chat.

Start a new conversation per problem, not per week

One conversation for the Monday exercises. One for the mini-project build. One for debugging. When a conversation gets too long or drifts off track, start a fresh one. The Project instructions and the uploaded files carry over. The conversation history does not.

Rule of thumb: if you've been in a conversation for more than 30 messages and the quality is dropping, start fresh.

The Five Skills, Applied

Every week drills one or more of these. By Week 12 you've repped all five two or three times.

1. Translate business problem to data question

You don't ask Claude "write me a SQL query for sales." You ask it the real question.

Bad: "Write a query for accounts."

Good: "I have a transactions table with account_id, transaction_date, balance_change. I need to find accounts where the daily running balance dipped below zero at any point in Q2 2025. Write the SQL, explain each clause, and tell me what edge cases to watch for."

The good version does the work of knowing what you're actually looking for. Claude can write the SQL. You have to know the question.

2. Command AI to execute

Prompts are commands, not wishes. Four parts:

  1. Context — what you're working with, what the data looks like.
  2. Task — what you want.
  3. Format — what the answer should look like (code? table? written summary?).
  4. Gotchas — what you think could go wrong.

Example:

I have a CSV of 2,300 bond trades from Q4 2024. Columns are trade_date, CUSIP, quantity, price. I need total dollar volume by month, ordered by month. Give me pandas code, add comments on each step, and flag any row where the price looks like an obvious typo.

That's a command. The AI has everything it needs.

3. Validate against domain knowledge

This is where treasury experience pays.

Claude will write perfectly valid SQL that gives you a wrong answer. The SQL is correct. The question you asked was incomplete. The numbers come back wrong and they don't match what you know about the business.

Always cross-check the first output against something you already know:

  • Does the total roughly match what you'd expect from memory?
  • Does the top-10 list include the names you know are big?
  • Is the time series smooth or does it have impossible spikes?

If something looks wrong, push back. Don't just re-prompt. Tell Claude specifically what doesn't match.

4. Iterate with AI

First answer is almost never the final answer. The work is the back and forth.

The July number is way off. Last I knew we did $40M in July, not $12M. What could cause that?

Claude will usually catch its own mistake. Missing a join, wrong date filter, dropping nulls that shouldn't be dropped. Your job is to know enough about the business to point it at the problem.

5. Present to non-technical stakeholders

Nobody cares about the SQL. Your boss wants to know what happened and what to do.

When the analysis is done, ask Claude to write two things:

  1. A one-page summary in plain English.
  2. The three things a decision maker should actually do with this.

Then rewrite both in your own words. Claude's summary is a starting draft. The interpretation has to come from you.

Good Pairing vs Bad Pairing

Bad pairing is asking Claude for an answer and taking it.

Good pairing is using Claude as a faster version of yourself, and checking its work.

What bad pairing looks like

You type: "What's the formula for IRR?"

Claude writes IRR. You copy it into Excel. You don't really know what you typed. Three weeks later you can't answer a question about it in an interview.

What good pairing looks like

You type: "Walk me through IRR conceptually, then show me the Excel formula. I want to understand why IRR is the discount rate that makes NPV zero before I use it on our data."

Claude explains. You rebuild the formula from the explanation. You run it against a project you already know the answer to. If it matches, you trust the tool. If it doesn't, you know you misunderstood something and you dig.

The difference isn't the output. Both get to the same IRR. The difference is that in the second case you learned something. In the first case you bought a number.

Cool Tricks

Context compounding

Put your best prompts and your domain facts in the Project instructions. Every conversation in the Project inherits them. After a few weeks your Project knows:

  • You're targeting treasury and FP&A analyst roles.
  • Your actual terminology (the real names you use for things, not textbook labels).
  • What you already know versus what's new.

After a month it feels less like typing a new prompt each time and more like working with someone who already knows you.

Upload files once, use them everywhere

Drop a reference doc into the Project (a course packet, a data dictionary, a sample output you're trying to match) and Claude can see it in every conversation inside that Project. You don't re-upload per chat. This alone saves you hours across the course.

The two-conversation pattern

One conversation to think. One conversation to build.

When you're sorting out what to build, you ramble and explore. When you're building, you want focused output. Separate conversations keep both clean. When you move from thinking to building, summarize what you decided in the thinking chat and paste that into the build chat as the starting context.

Rubber duck debugging

When your own code is broken and you can't see why, paste it in and ask "what am I missing." You're not asking Claude to fix the code. You're asking it to tell you what question you should be asking. Often that's enough.

Artifacts as templates

Claude's artifacts save inside the conversation. Build a SQL template once (a "get the monthly P&L" pattern, say) and save it somewhere you can grab later. Your templates accumulate into your personal tool library over the twelve weeks.

The "explain like I'm five" trick

When you read Claude's output and something feels off, ask "explain this to me like I'm five." If Claude can't explain it simply, the answer is probably wrong or Claude doesn't really know. If Claude can explain it, you'll usually catch the error in the explanation itself.

Ask for two answers

When you're not sure what the right approach is, ask "give me two different ways to solve this and tell me the tradeoffs." You learn faster comparing two options than accepting one.

The daily log

End of every day you work, spend 5 minutes telling Claude what you learned today, what confused you, and what you want to come back to tomorrow. Tomorrow's conversation starts with context. Also: by Week 12 you have a written record of everything you learned. That record is the draft of your LinkedIn case studies.

The Cowork Hour

One hour with me, every Sunday, end of your week.

The format

  1. You walk me through what you built. 20 minutes. Screen share. Open the Claude conversation. Show me the prompts you used and the answers you got. Show me the portfolio entry.
  2. I ask questions. 20 minutes. Not trick questions. Real ones. What did this query do. Why did you pick this chart. What would you change if the data doubled.
  3. We plan next week. 15 minutes. Look at the packet. Flag what looks hard. Decide where you want help.
  4. Five minutes at the end. Anything else. Life stuff. Questions about the industry. Whatever.

If you can't make Sunday, we reschedule. Not skip.

When It Falls Apart

Sometimes the pair stops working. Claude keeps giving you confident wrong answers. You're copy-pasting without understanding. You're burning an hour and getting nothing useful.

When that happens, close the laptop. Walk away. Come back with a narrower question.

Or: skip Claude for an hour. Pen and paper. Draw the tables. Write the query longhand. Then check your work against Claude. Sometimes your brain needs to run the problem first.

The goal is not to use Claude for everything. The goal is to be dangerous with both the AI and the thinking behind it.

A Short Checklist Before Every Session

Before you open Claude, ask yourself these three things:

  1. What am I actually trying to figure out? One sentence. If you can't say it in one sentence, you're not ready to prompt.
  2. What do I already know about the answer? Write down what the result should roughly look like. This is your validation check.
  3. What am I going to do with the answer once I have it? If you don't know, you're doing busywork.

30 seconds of this before you start saves you an hour of random prompting.
