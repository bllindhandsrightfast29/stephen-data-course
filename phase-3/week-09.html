Week 9 — Portfolio Assembly

Phase 3 · Visualization Skill Focus: Translate + Present (combined) Job Search Kicks Off ~14 hours
Course Progress
Week 9 of 12

You have eight portfolio pieces. This week you shape them into a portfolio a recruiter will actually click through, rebuild your LinkedIn, update your resume, and fire off your first 10 applications. Phase 4 (Python) still runs, but the job search is now running alongside it. The point from here is not to finish the course. The point is to get a job.

Learning Goals

  • Polish eight portfolio entries into a coherent portfolio README that tells a story
  • Rebuild LinkedIn profile with a data-analyst-facing headline, About section, and Featured items
  • Update resume to one page, skills-first, with the course projects as experience
  • Submit 10 job applications by Saturday
  • Do 2 mock interviews with Claude (SQL technical + behavioral)

Why This Week Is Different

Weeks 1-8 were about building. This week is about packaging.

A treasury analyst with 15 years of finance experience plus a completed data analyst course is a different candidate than someone starting from scratch. But only if the portfolio tells that story clearly. Recruiters spend 30 seconds scanning a resume and 2 minutes on a portfolio. Everything has to land fast.

You'll also start applying this week. Not after the course ends. The typical mistake is waiting until “everything is perfect.” Nothing is perfect, and the capstone in Weeks 11-12 will be stronger if you already have interview feedback by then.

The Week in Four Tracks

Track 1 — Portfolio Polish

Audit your eight portfolio entries (Weeks 1-8). Rewrite each README to be recruiter-readable. Build a top-level portfolio README that tells the story.

Track 2 — LinkedIn Rebuild

New headline, new About section, Featured items linking to your three strongest case studies, skills section refreshed with the certs you earned.

Track 3 — Resume Update

One page. Skills at the top. Course projects treated as experience. The shape is different from a traditional resume for a reason.

Track 4 — First Applications

10 submissions by Saturday. Plus 2 mock interviews with Claude. You're in the market now.

AI Co-Work Pattern — Translate + Present Combined

The portfolio-framing drill

The hardest part of portfolio writing is figuring out the one-sentence takeaway for each project. That's Skill #1 (translate) and Skill #5 (present) working together.

For every portfolio piece, spend 10 minutes with Claude answering these three questions:

  1. What business problem did this solve? (one sentence, plain English, no jargon)
  2. What did the analysis find? (one sentence with the specific insight, not “analyzed data”)
  3. What would you do differently or next if you had more time? (shows self-awareness)

Those three sentences become the top of every README. Everything else is supporting detail.

Track 1 — Portfolio Polish

Step 1: Audit what you have

Open your stephen-data-portfolio repo. Go through each week folder. For each:

  • Does the README have the three-sentence framing at the top?
  • Is the code commented clearly?
  • Are broken links cleaned up?
  • Are screenshots visible in GitHub's renderer (not broken image links)?

Make a list of what needs fixing. Fix in order of how proud you are of each piece.

Step 2: The portfolio-level README

The README.md at the root of your portfolio repo is the first thing anyone sees. It should have:

  1. A 3-sentence intro — who you are, what you can do, what this portfolio shows
  2. A skills section — the tools you know, at what level, with a brief note on each (not a bullet list of 30 things)
  3. The five strongest pieces — not all eight. Each with a one-line description and a link
  4. The certs earned — with links to verify
  5. Contact info — LinkedIn, email

Build it with Claude

Here's my portfolio folder structure: [list your folders]
Here are the five strongest pieces I want to feature: [list them]
My target roles are: Treasury Analyst, FP&A Analyst, Revenue Ops
Analyst at fintechs or F500 companies.

Help me draft a portfolio-level README that tells a story. Not a
checklist. A story: what I've been doing, what I can do, what I'm
looking for. Keep it under 400 words total. Direct, no fluff.

First pass is yours. Then I'll rewrite in my voice.

Track 2 — LinkedIn Rebuild

The headline

You get 220 characters. Use them. Candidates fail here by writing “Seeking opportunities” or “Passionate about data.” Don't.

Format: [What you do] | [Domain expertise] | [Specific tools]

Examples to steal from, not copy:

  • “Data Analyst transitioning from 15 years of Treasury | SQL, Python, Looker Studio | Specializing in FP&A analytics”
  • “Treasury Analyst with Applied Data Analytics skills | Pair-working with AI to ship faster | Open to roles”

Use your own words. The goal is specific enough that a recruiter searching for “treasury analyst SQL” finds you.

The About section (the real one)

2000 characters. Three paragraphs max.

  1. Paragraph 1: who you are and what you've been doing (treasury, 15 years, the specific work)
  2. Paragraph 2: the transition — what you've built with data tooling, specific projects, specific results
  3. Paragraph 3: what you're looking for now, what kind of role, call to action

Don't write it in third person. Don't write it generic. It should sound like you talking, not like a LinkedIn template.

Featured items

LinkedIn lets you feature 3-4 links at the top of your profile. Use them for:

  1. Your published Looker Studio dashboard (from Week 8)
  2. Your portfolio README on GitHub
  3. Your Customer 360 SQL case study (Week 5)

These are what get clicked. Make sure they look good.

Skills section

Add the specific tools you've used: Excel, Google Sheets, SQL, PostgreSQL, Looker Studio, Google Analytics, Python (if you want to add it now), pandas. Add your treasury-domain skills too: Cash Flow Analysis, Working Capital Management, Financial Reporting. Don't oversell. Don't undersell.

Track 3 — Resume Update

The shape

One page. Single column. Skills at the top, not the bottom.

  1. Name + one-line summary + contact (4 lines)
  2. Skills section — Tools / Domain / Certifications (3 sub-rows, comma separated)
  3. Recent Projects — top 3 from your portfolio, each with a 2-line description and the metrics or deliverable
  4. Experience — your last 2-3 treasury/accounting roles, reframed to highlight analytical work you already did
  5. Education + certs — the Kaggle + CFI + Looker Studio certs here

The experience reframe

You've been doing data analysis work for years. Pull out the quantitative bullets. Use Claude:

I'm updating my resume's experience section. Here's how I
described my Treasury Analyst role at [company]:

[paste current bullets]

Rewrite these bullets to emphasize the analytical and data-driven
parts of the work. Each bullet should:
- Start with an action verb (Analyzed, Built, Modeled, Reduced,
  Identified)
- Contain a specific number or measurable outcome
- Tie to the kind of work a data analyst does

Give me 6 bullet options. I'll pick 3-4.

Track 4 — First Applications + Mock Interviews

Target role list

Before you apply, build a target list. 20 companies you'd actually want to work at. Use these filters on LinkedIn Jobs or Indeed:

  • “Treasury Analyst,” “FP&A Analyst,” “Revenue Operations Analyst,” “Senior Financial Analyst”
  • Filter to “entry-level” OR “mid-level” based on years of experience they're asking for (ignore job-title level)
  • Salary range $100K-$160K if visible
  • Remote OR hybrid OR your city

Don't apply yet. Build the list first. Prioritize.

The applications

10 applications by Saturday. Each takes ~30 minutes if you tailor the resume. Use Claude to tailor:

I'm applying to [specific role] at [specific company]. Here's the
job description: [paste]. Here's my current resume: [paste].

Tell me:
1. The 3 skills/experiences from my resume that most match this
   role. These get emphasized.
2. Anything on my resume that doesn't match or is noise. Trim.
3. A custom one-line summary for the top of this tailored version.
4. Any language from the job description that should appear in my
   resume bullets (keyword matching).

I'll do the rewriting. You identify the gaps.

Mock interviews

Two with Claude this week. One technical (SQL), one behavioral.

Technical prompt:

Conduct a 30-minute SQL technical interview for a treasury analyst
role. Ask me 5 questions of increasing difficulty (start with
SELECT/WHERE, end with window functions). For each:

1. Give me the question as a hiring manager would
2. Wait for my answer
3. Give feedback on my answer: what was right, what was wrong,
   what I missed
4. Then move to the next question

At the end, rate my SQL interview-readiness 1-10 and tell me what
to drill before the real interview.

Behavioral prompt:

Conduct a 30-minute behavioral interview for a treasury analyst
role. Ask me 6 common behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time
you made a mistake," "Describe your most complex analysis," etc.).

For each:
1. Ask the question
2. Wait for my STAR-format answer (Situation/Task/Action/Result)
3. Feedback: did my answer use STAR, was it specific enough, did
   it highlight the skills relevant to the role

At the end, tell me which of my answers needed work and suggest
stronger story angles I might use in the real interview.

Mini-Project — The Whole Package

Deliverable this week is not one thing but three:

  1. Polished portfolio repo at github.com/[your-username]/stephen-data-portfolio with a strong top-level README
  2. Rebuilt LinkedIn profile with new headline, About, Featured items, Skills
  3. Updated 1-page resume saved as PDF, ready to send

Plus: 10 applications submitted, 2 mock interviews completed with Claude.

Commit Saturday

Push the portfolio updates. Save resume as resume_stephen_branch_2026.pdf in the root of the portfolio repo. Update LinkedIn live. Track your 10 applications in a simple spreadsheet (date applied, company, role, status).

How This Builds Forward

Weeks 10-12 run the Python track in parallel with the job search. You'll keep applying, keep interviewing, keep polishing. The capstone in Week 12 becomes your strongest portfolio piece and probably the first thing you mention in interviews. By the time the course ends, you should have at least a few first-round interviews under your belt and know which technical gaps to close.
