Week 7 — Dashboard Fundamentals

Phase 3 · Visualization Skill Focus: Present to Non-Technical Stakeholders ~10 hours
Course Progress
Week 7 of 12

This week is conceptual. You take a week off from writing code and learn how to decide what chart to use, what a bad dashboard looks like, and how to design something an executive can read in five seconds. Next week you build a real dashboard in Looker Studio. This week you learn the principles that make that dashboard good.

Learning Goals

  • Know the eight chart types that cover 95% of business data viz, and when to use each
  • Recognize and avoid the common viz sins (chart junk, truncated axes, 3D, bad color)
  • Apply the five-second test and audience-first design principles
  • Drill Skill #5: present insight, not data
  • Ship a “chart redesign” portfolio piece taking 3 bad real-world charts and showing how you'd fix them

Why This Week Is Just Principles

Most people skip this week in their self-taught path. They jump straight into Tableau or Looker Studio, learn the mechanics, build ugly dashboards that technically work, and then can't figure out why nobody at work reads them.

The chart-design literature is small. Edward Tufte, Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic, Alberto Cairo. You can get the 80% in a week. You will use it every week for the rest of your career.

Claude is useful here as a design reviewer. You describe a chart you're considering. Claude tells you whether it's the right chart type, what to watch out for, and what an audience will actually read. You drill the decision-making, not the tool.

The Eight Chart Types You'll Actually Use

  1. Bar chart (horizontal) — comparing categories. Default choice.
  2. Column chart (vertical bar) — comparing categories over time (months, quarters)
  3. Line chart — a value changing continuously over time
  4. Area chart — cumulative or stacked value over time
  5. Scatter plot — relationship between two variables
  6. Heatmap — a matrix of values where color conveys magnitude
  7. Big number + sparkline — a KPI plus its recent trend
  8. Table (well-formatted) — when exact numbers matter, like a 10-row ranked list

That's it. Pie charts are almost always worse than bar charts. 3D anything is always worse. Treemaps and bubble charts have narrow use cases. Start with the eight above.

AI Co-Work Pattern — Skill #5: Present Insight, Not Data

The chart critique drill

For every chart you consider building, run this pre-build check with Claude:

I'm considering a chart with these details:

- Data: [what fields and grain]
- Question it answers: [in one sentence]
- Audience: [treasury analyst, CFO, general executive]
- Time constraint: [how long do they have to read it]

I'm thinking of using a [chart type]. Critique the choice:
1. Is this the right chart type for this question and audience?
2. What's a better option if not?
3. What would break if the data doubled in rows?
4. What's the one-line takeaway a reader should get in 5 seconds?

If you can't write the one-line takeaway, the chart isn't worth building. That's the real test.

The Five Drills

Drill 1 — Chart type matching

I'll describe 10 business questions. Tell me which of the 8 chart
types fits each best, and why. If more than one could work, tell
me which is better and when you'd pick the alternative.

1. “How has monthly revenue changed over the past 2 years?”
2. “Which 10 customers contribute the most to Q4 revenue?”
3. “Is there a relationship between customer age and purchase size?”
4. “What is our current cash balance?”
5. “How does transaction volume vary by hour of day and day of week?”
6. “What's the breakdown of revenue across 5 product lines in Q3?”
7. “How did our 3 regions perform month-over-month last year?”
8. “Who are our top 10 most-overdue customers, with amounts?”
9. “Is our customer satisfaction score trending up or down?”
10. “How is our AR aging distributed across buckets?”

Be opinionated. Don't hedge.

Drill 2 — Viz sin identification

Search Google Images for “bad data visualization” and pick 5
examples. Upload screenshots.

For each one, tell me:
1. What the chart is trying to show
2. What's wrong with it (could be multiple things)
3. The specific viz sin it's committing (by name: chart junk,
   truncated axis, 3D distortion, bad color scaling, too many
   series, misleading scale, etc.)
4. How you'd redesign it in 1-2 sentences

Drill 3 — Color theory basics

Walk me through color choices for data viz. Specifically:

1. When to use a sequential color scale vs diverging vs categorical
2. How many distinct colors is the max before viewers can't
   differentiate (hint: 5-7)
3. Why colorblind accessibility matters and how to check it
4. Which colors have implicit meaning (red = bad, green = good,
   blue = neutral) and when to lean into that vs fight it
5. The ColorBrewer tool (colorbrewer2.org) — explain what it
   does and when to use it

Then: given a treasury dashboard showing 4 customer segments,
what colors would you pick and why?

Drill 4 — The five-second test

The five-second test: if an executive glances at your dashboard
for 5 seconds, what should they walk away knowing?

Walk me through applying the test to a hypothetical treasury
dashboard with 8 charts. Specifically:

1. What goes in the top-left (the eye starts there)?
2. What goes in the big number position?
3. What's the risk of having 8 charts (cognitive overload) and
   how to reduce it?
4. Give me the 3-part dashboard layout: overview, mid-level,
   detail. How should the tabs of a dashboard be organized?

Then: design the wireframe (described in text, not drawn) for a
one-page executive treasury dashboard that passes the 5-second
test.

Drill 5 — The one-line takeaway drill

For each of these 5 business scenarios, write ONE sentence that
captures the insight. Then tell me what chart type would best
support that sentence (not illustrate everything about the data,
just support the one sentence).

1. Q4 revenue missed target by 12%, driven entirely by the
   Enterprise segment underperforming.
2. Customer satisfaction has been steady at 4.2/5 for six quarters,
   but complaints about billing have tripled in the past 3 months.
3. 30% of our transaction volume comes from 5 customers, making
   us concentration-risky.
4. Cash conversion cycle has improved from 45 to 32 days, driven
   by faster AR collection.
5. We've added 200 customers but lost 50 of our top-tier accounts.
   Customer count is up; revenue is down.

The exercise trains Skill #5: you build a chart to support one
sentence, not to be comprehensive.

Mini-Project — Chart Redesign Case Study

Deliverable: a markdown case study with 3 bad real-world charts and your redesigned versions, shipped to portfolio. This is a pure viz-design deliverable, no tool-building required.

How to do it

  1. Find 3 bad charts from real sources. Options: a company earnings slide deck (SEC filings have them), a government report, a magazine infographic. Must be real, must be publicly sourced.
  2. For each, write a short critique: what's wrong, what viz sin, what the audience can't see
  3. Redesign each in your tool of choice: hand-sketched (photo of paper), whiteboard, or Looker Studio prototype (even a screenshot of a chart with no real data behind it works)
  4. Write a 2-paragraph reflection: what you learned from doing this

How to build it with Claude

Upload each bad chart to your Week 7 Claude Project. Run the critique drill (Drill 2 pattern) on it. Have Claude help you draft the redesign description. Then you actually redesign it yourself and save the visual.

Portfolio entry

Push to stephen-data-portfolio/week_07_chart_redesign/:

  • README.md — your case study, 3 sections (one per chart), with embedded before-and-after images
  • images/ — the original bad charts plus your redesigns

This piece demonstrates taste, not technical skill. That matters for senior roles.
